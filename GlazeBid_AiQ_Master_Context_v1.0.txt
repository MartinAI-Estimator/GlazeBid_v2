The MissionGlazeBid AiQ is an automated drawing intelligence engine built specifically for commercial glazing estimators. Its purpose is to read architectural PDF drawing sets and automatically identify, classify, and measure glazing scope — replacing the manual takeoff process that currently takes days and falls entirely on the estimator's expertise and time.The core problem: A glazing contractor receives a set of architectural drawings and must manually identify every piece of glass and aluminum framing, determine system types, cross-reference details and schedules, and produce a scope list accurate enough to bid from. This process takes 8-40 hours per project depending on complexity. It requires deep domain expertise. And it has to be done over and over, for every bid, by the same person.GlazeBid AiQ solves this by encoding the reading process — the same cognitive sequence an experienced glazing estimator uses — into a deterministic software system that runs locally on the estimator's machine.1.1 The Proof of ConceptOn April 8, 2026, a real-world drawing test was conducted. Two complete architectural drawing sets were provided:McLarty Mazda of Little Rock — commercial auto dealership, BCO+H ArchitectsHope Aquatic and Recreation Center — City of Hope, Arkansas — BiLD ArchitectsWithout any project-specific training, the system correctly identified:All exterior curtainwall and storefront systems including manufacturer, finish, and glass specificationInterior storefront and frameless glass office partition locationsSystem type distinctions (curtainwall vs. storefront vs. translucent panel)Door schedule glazing types and frame systemsScope misses were not reading failures — they were scope profile gaps. The system could see the OH doors. It just did not know the contractor does not pick them up. The system could see the translucent panels. It did not know they are included in the glazing contractor's scope on this type of project.Critical finding: The information is readable. The logic is sound. The gap between what was found and what was marked is a business rules problem, not a geometry problem. This validates the entire architecture.1.2 The Strategic VisionGlazeBid AiQ is not just an estimating tool. It is the foundation for a platform that could transform how the entire glazing industry operates. The same reading architecture that works for glazing works for every specialty subcontractor. The same scope profile system that works for one glazing company works for all of them.The long-term vision: every specialty trade has a version of this system, all built on the same foundation, all learning from each other's project data. The contractor who builds it first owns the methodology, not just the product.2. Critical Decisions and Why They Were MadeThese are not preferences — they are conclusions reached through explicit analysis. Future development must understand the reasoning behind each decision before reconsidering it.2.1 Rules-Based, Not Machine LearningThe original design in the GlazeBid AiQ Implementation Plan (Version 1.0, March 2026) included an ML pipeline built on Bluebeam markup data as training labels. This was abandoned for two reasons:Bluebeam markups are contaminated with human judgment. Estimator markups document bid scope decisions — what was included, what was excluded, information-only callouts — not just glazing geometry. A model trained on these markups would learn estimating decisions, not glazing physics. The signal and the noise are inseparable.Glazing geometry follows physical laws. Mullions must be parallel. Glass lites must form closed rectangles. Dimensions must fall within manufacturable ranges. These are not statistical patterns — they are physical constraints. A rules engine enforcing physical constraints is more defensible, more explainable, and faster to build than a model trying to learn the same constraints from noisy data.Decision: The system is rules-based. Physical constraints from the GANA Glazing Manual are encoded as explicit rules. The ML layers (Architectural BERT, EGNN, Physics-Informed GNN) from Version 1.0 are removed from the implementation plan. They may be revisited in Phase 3 as refinement layers only.2.2 Local Sidecar, Not Cloud ServiceThe system runs as a local Python FastAPI sidecar service alongside the GlazeBid Electron app on localhost:8100. No architectural drawings leave the contractor's machine. This is non-negotiable for the target market — glazing contractors will not send project drawings to a cloud service.2.3 PDF Normalization Before AnalysisArchitectural drawings vary enormously in drafting conventions, line weight usage, layer organization, and visual noise. Rather than building a rule engine that handles all this variation, the system preprocesses elevation sheets by stripping all stroke weight variation and redrawing paths at uniform weight. This dramatically simplifies the rule battery.Important: Normalization is sheet-type conditional. Elevation sheets get full normalization. Floor plan sheets preserve relative line weights because the cut plane distinction (thick = cut element, thin = beyond cut plane) is important for identifying opening symbols. Detail sheets preserve all attributes.2.4 The Scope Profile is Separate from the Detection EngineThe system must separate three distinct problems that previous approaches conflated:ProblemQuestionSolutionDetectionIs this a glazing assembly?Rules engine — geometry + physics constraintsClassificationWhat kind of glazing assembly is it?System identification — schedule parsing + geometryScopingIs this in our scope?Scope profile — company-specific business rulesThe scope profile is a company-level configuration. It has three tiers: industry standard defaults (what most glazing contractors pick up), company-level overrides (simple toggles for common scope boundary differences), and conditional rules (size thresholds, floor level, orientation). The profile learns over time from human reviewer decisions in the confirmation interface.3. Understanding this grammar is prerequisite to reading any drawing correctly. The key principles encoded in the system:Orthographic ProjectionAll views in a drawing set are geometrically related through orthographic projection. Height information is consistent between front and side views (horizontal alignment). Width information is consistent between front and top views (vertical alignment). The system uses these relationships to cross-reference candidates across sheets.The Five View Types and Their RolesView TypeSheet PrefixRole in Glazing TakeoffFloor PlanA2.xOpening locations, widths, wall thickness, column grid references, door/window tagsElevationA4.xSystem types, heights, overall glazing percentage, visual confirmationWall SectionA5.xHead/sill/jamb conditions, glass bite, anchor conditions, vertical dimensionsDetailA3.xExact connection geometry, system type confirmation, frame profile identificationScheduleA3.x / A8.xSystem names, manufacturer specs, unit counts, sizes, hardware setsThe Reading SequenceThis sequence is the algorithm. It is defined by the blueprint reading training curriculum and validated by 15 years of estimating experience. Center lines (alternating long/short dash) are the mullion dimension reference points on curtainwall drawings.3.2 Layer 0B — Glazing Physics (GANA Glazing Manual)The GANA Glazing Manual, 50th Anniversary Edition, is the industry standard reference for glazing physics. System Architecture4.1 How the Estimator's Brain Works — The Reading ModelThe system is designed to mimic how an experienced glazing estimator reads a drawing set. This is not a metaphor — it is the literal architecture.Key insight from field validation: An estimator opening a new project does not discover scope — they confirm it. They form an expectation of scope category based on building type before opening a single drawing. The drawings answer the how questions (dimensions, manufacturer, detail conditions), not the what questions (glazing is here). The system works the same way: expectation-first, confirmation-second, exception-third.Building type classification sets the prior expectation before geometry analysis runsSchedule parsing builds the named scope inventory from text before elevation geometry is analyzedElevation geometry confirms and locates the expected inventory — it does not discover from scratchDetail cross-referencing validates system type and extracts connection geometryPhysics validation filters false positives using GANA constraintsScope profile converts detected candidates to contractor scope decisions4.2 The Three-Problem StackThe system addresses three separate problems. Previous automated takeoff approaches failed by treating them as one problem.Problem 1 — Detection: Is this a glazing assembly? Solved by the rules engine applying physical constraints from the GANA manual to vector geometry extracted from the PDF.Problem 2 — Classification: What type of glazing assembly is it? Solved by system identification logic combining schedule text parsing with geometric signatures.Problem 3 — Scoping: Is this assembly in the contractor's scope? Solved by the scope profile layer applying company-specific business rules to classified candidates.4.3 Layer ArchitectureThe complete layer stack from raw PDF to confirmed scope:#Layer NameTechnologyFunction0ADrawing GrammarEncoded rulesSheet type classification, view relationships, reference bubble parsing, scale detection0BGlazing PhysicsEncoded rulesGANA constraints — dimensional limits, bite requirements, deflection tolerances, system type definitions0CScope ProfileConfig fileCompany-specific include/exclude rules, tiered from industry defaults to conditional geometry rules1Sheet RouterOCR + NLPReads title block, classifies sheet type, routes to appropriate processing pipeline2Vector Graph ExtractionPyMuPDFConverts PDF paths to planar graph — nodes at intersections, edges as segments with attributes. The score determines routing:ConfidenceActionUser Interface0.90 – 1.00Auto-acceptGreen — confirmed automatically0.70 – 0.89Suggest — human confirms in 1 clickYellow — review recommended0.50 – 0.69Flag — human must decideOrange — decision required0.00 – 0.49RejectRed — excluded from scopeThe Centaur Model: 80-88% of scope is auto-accepted by the system. Every candidate carries scale_factor and scale_confidence. If scale confidence is low, dimensional feasibility checks (Tier 1 Rule 1.3) are skipped — they would check nonsense numbers. The system attempts scale detection in this order:Graphic scale bar — horizontal line with tick marks and nearby text containing a number and unitDimension string correlation — find line segments with nearby numeric annotations, compute ratio from multiple matching stringsTitle block OCR — search for 'SCALE:' field in title blockIf all three fail, scale_confidence returns 0.0 and the candidate is flagged for manual scale calibration. Manual calibration on one sheet propagates to all sheets of the same type via the cross-reference homography.4.7 The Anti-Hallucination FrameworkThe system NEVER outputs a dimension it has not extracted directly from the drawing — no interpolationThe system NEVER claims 100% confidence — maximum reported confidence is capped at 0.97Every candidate carries a provenance chain — which layers contributed, which sheets were cross-referencedIf layers disagree, lower confidence wins and item is flaggedUnknown conditions (confidence < 0.50) are always escalated to human review — never auto-acceptedAll model outputs are logged with timestamps for post-project audit5. It has not been solved for four reasons:The market is invisible to Silicon Valley. Specialty subcontractors are not a venture capital target. The pain is real but the industry does not speak the language of tech investment.Drawings look structured but are not. No enforced standards, no consistent layer naming, hand-drafted details mixed with BIM exports. The signal is real but buried under inconsistent drafting conventions.The people who understand the drawings cannot build the software. The people who can build the software do not understand the drawings. That gap has never been closed.Previous approaches attacked the problem from the wrong direction — software engineers trying to learn construction documents, rather than construction experts encoding what they know into software.Martin has 15 years of experience as a field glazier, PM, project engineer, and estimator. He has been on both ends of the problem — waiting on takeoff as a PM, and working from a bad takeoff as a field glazier. He is the first person with the domain expertise AND the motivation to solve this from the right direction.9.2 The Competitive MoatThe scope profile system — built from real contractor behavior across hundreds of companies — becomes a dataset of how the glazing industry actually works. Nobody else can build this without the project history. It gets more valuable with every company that onboards.The closed loop: GlazeBid generates shop drawings in a normalized geometric format. That same format is what the reading engine targets. Every shop drawing GlazeBid generates becomes a perfect training example for future reading improvements. The output format IS the canonical representation the rule engine targets.9.3 Adjacent MarketsThe same reading architecture and methodology applies to every specialty subcontractor who reads architectural drawings for scope. Mechanical (duct layouts, equipment), electrical (panels, conduit, devices), plumbing (fixtures, routing), masonry (opening counts, lintel spans), steel erectors (connection details, member schedules). The framework is tradeable. The domain knowledge layer is what changes per trade.10.
